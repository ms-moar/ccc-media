# Phase 4: Differentiation Analysis

> **CUSTOMIZATION GUIDE**: This template provides a framework for identifying what makes your proposed contribution unique and valuable compared to existing alternatives. Adapt the questions and criteria below to fit your specific research domain.

## How to Customize This Phase
- Replace "solution" with the appropriate term (contribution, approach, methodology, offering, innovation)
- Adjust competitive comparison to fit your context (prior research, alternative methods, existing options)
- Modify value proposition language based on your field (novel contribution, unique advantage, key innovation)
- Scale defensibility assessment to match your domain's criteria

---

## Objective
Identify what makes your proposed approach unique and valuable compared to existing alternatives. Define your distinctive contribution and positioning.

## Required Questions

### 4.1 Unique Value Proposition
- What would your approach do that others don't?
- Why would someone choose this over existing alternatives?
- What is the core differentiator?

### 4.2 Sustainable Advantages
- What sustainable advantages could this approach have?
- Is this differentiation defensible over time?
- How easily could others replicate this?

### 4.3 Target Audience
- Who specifically would value this differentiation most?
- Is there an underserved group you could focus on?
- What specific niche could you address initially?

### 4.4 Positioning
- How would you position this against alternatives?
- What category does this belong in (or does it create a new one)?
- What is the one-sentence description of your unique contribution?

### 4.5 Trade-offs
- What are you choosing NOT to do?
- What aspects would you intentionally deprioritize?
- What compromises enable your differentiation?

## Validation Criteria

### PASS if:
- [ ] Clear, specific differentiation is identified
- [ ] Differentiation addresses real needs from Phase 1
- [ ] Differentiation leverages gaps found in Phase 2
- [ ] Differentiation is feasible per Phase 3
- [ ] There is a specific audience who would strongly value this

### FAIL if:
- [ ] Differentiation is vague (e.g., "better" without specifics)
- [ ] Differentiation is easily replicable by existing players
- [ ] Differentiation doesn't matter to the target audience
- [ ] No specific group would strongly prefer this
- [ ] Trying to appeal to everyone without focus

## Differentiation Strategies

Consider these types of differentiation:
1. **Novelty** - New approach, method, or insight others lack
2. **Quality** - Significantly better execution or rigor
3. **Efficiency** - Faster, cheaper, or more accessible
4. **Scope** - Broader or deeper coverage than alternatives
5. **Integration** - Better fits into existing workflows or knowledge
6. **Focus** - Purpose-built for a specific use case or audience
7. **Perspective** - Different philosophical or methodological approach

## Domain-Specific Differentiation

| Research Domain | Common Differentiators | Defensibility Sources |
|-----------------|------------------------|----------------------|
| Academic | Novel method, new data, unique perspective | Expertise, data access, citations |
| Market Research | Better data, faster delivery, deeper insights | Proprietary data, methodology |
| Scientific | New discovery, improved method, broader scope | Patent, expertise, equipment |
| Business | Better value, unique features, superior experience | Brand, network, technology |
| Policy | Stronger evidence, broader coalition, better framing | Relationships, credibility |

## Tips for This Phase

1. Differentiation must matter to your target audience - not just be different
2. Test your positioning with actual members of the target audience
3. Be specific about trade-offs - trying to be everything often means being nothing
4. Consider both current differentiation and future defensibility
5. The strongest differentiation often comes from saying "no" to certain things
